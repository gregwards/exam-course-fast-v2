# Resource 1: Learning from Failures

For **Assignment 2: Outbreak Post-Mortem Analysis** (Step 1), review these two outbreak examples to understand what information matters when analyzing food service failures. You cannot use these outbreaks for your assignment, but they show you what to look for in the case you select.

## Example 1: Chipotle's Multi-State Outbreaks (2015)

Between October and December 2015, Chipotle Mexican Grill dealt with multiple foodborne illness outbreaks across their restaurant locations. The company faced E. coli, norovirus, and Salmonella cases that sickened over 500 people and led to temporary store closures in nine states.

### What Went Wrong?

**Supplier Management Failures:** Chipotle's business model emphasized fresh, locally-sourced ingredients. Individual restaurants sourced from local suppliers who weren't held to consistent safety standards. When contaminated produce entered the system, there was no centralized testing or verification process to catch it before it reached customers.

**Decentralized Food Preparation:** Unlike most fast-food chains that prepare food in central commissaries under controlled conditions, Chipotle restaurants did all food prep on-site. This created more opportunities for cross-contamination and less consistency in safety protocols across hundreds of locations. Each restaurant essentially operated its own small food production facility without the quality control systems you'd find in a commercial processing plant.

**Temperature Control Issues:** Investigators found that cold foods weren't consistently held at safe temperatures. Chipotle's open kitchen layout and high-volume service meant food sat in the prep line for extended periods. When food enters the danger zone (41-135°F), bacteria can multiply to dangerous levels within hours.

**Training Gaps:** High employee turnover and rushed onboarding meant food safety protocols weren't consistently followed. Proper handwashing procedures, glove use, and cross-contamination prevention weren't embedded into daily operations. You can have excellent written procedures, but if staff don't understand why they matter or how to implement them correctly, they're just paper.

### What Were the Consequences?

Over 1,100 illnesses were documented across 14 states. Sickening thousands of customers had a severe impact on customer's trust in Chipotle. Stores closed across the country.

Chipotle saw their stock drop 44% in six months, and faced federal criminal charges and a $25 million fine.

## Example 2: E. coli Outbreak (2022)

Read the full CDC investigation: [CDC E. coli O157:H7 Outbreak Investigation](https://archive.cdc.gov/www_cdc_gov/ecoli/2022/o157h7-08-22/index.html)

In August 2022, an E. coli O157:H7 outbreak sickened 109 people, with 52 hospitalizations and 13 cases of hemolytic uremic syndrome (a serious kidney condition). The October 4, 2022 CDC update declared the outbreak over before they could confirm the source of the outbreak, but more than 80% of sick people reported eating romaine lettuce at Wendy's before developing symptoms.

### What Were the Consequences?

In this case, the root causes remained undetermined. [Wendy's still paid millions of dollars in settlements](https://www.mlive.com/news/2025/09/wendys-pays-28-million-to-michigan-residents-over-2022-ecoli-outbreak.html) from lawsuits related to the 2022 E. coli outbreak.

## How to Analyze Outbreaks

When you analyze your chosen outbreak, search for the following information:

1. What was the food vehicle? (contaminated ingredient, finished product, etc.)
2. Where did contamination originate? (supplier, in-house handling, environmental)
3. What operational breakdowns allowed it to reach consumers? (receiving procedures, temperature control, cross-contamination, training gaps)
4. How long did it continue before detection? (surveillance and response systems)
5. What specific management changes would have prevented this? (not generic "better training" — rather, what specific training, procedures, monitoring).

Read your CDC outbreak report looking for these operational details, not just the pathogen facts.
