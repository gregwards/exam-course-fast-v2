# Resource 2: Training Posters That Actually Work

For **Assignment 1: Cross-Contamination Training Poster**, review this resource before you start designing. It will help you create a poster that employees read and remember.

## Why Training Posters Are Everywhere

Walk into any commercial kitchen, hospital cafeteria, or school lunch line and you'll find posters on the walls. Handwashing steps above every sink. Temperature charts on cooler doors. Allergen lists at prep stations.

These posters exist because they work. A well-placed reminder at the point of action catches people exactly when they need it. Health departments expect to see them. Managers use them to reinforce training. During busy service, when there's no time to consult a manual, a poster provides instant reference.

Your assignment puts you in the position of a food service manager responding to an incident. Something went wrong, and you need to make sure staff don't repeat the mistake. A training poster is one of the most direct tools you have.

## What Makes a Poster Effective?

### Focus on one clear message

The chopping board color chart does exactly one thing: it shows which color board goes with which food type. Raw meat is red. Vegetables are green. Allergens are purple. A new employee in a commercial kitchen immediately knows what boards to use for each food type, and does their part to prevent cross contamination.

### Show the correct action

This handwashing posters shows how to do it properly: wet hands, apply soap, scrub for 20 seconds, rinse, dry. An employee can look at it mid-task and follow along. Strong posters make the correct actions obvious at a glance.

### Write for someone in a rush

Your audience is a line cook during dinner service. They have maybe three seconds to absorb your poster while moving between tasks.

This means designing posters with:

- Short phrases
- Numbered steps or bullet points
- Large text for key actions
- Visual hierarchy that guides the eye to what matters most

### Connect to a concrete scenario

The "Would You Want These People In Your Kitchen" poster isn't just attention-grabbing. It makes it extremely clear (even a bit uncomfortable) to look at people who are very sick and should not be working in a kitchen. It's direct and concrete: if you have these symptoms, you need to stay home.

## More Examples Worth Studying

[FDA Retail Food Protection Posters](https://www.fda.gov/food/retail-food-industryregulatory-assistance-training/retail-food-protection-industry-educational-materials): Free, downloadable posters on handwashing, cross-contamination prevention, and temperature control. Available in multiple languages. These use a "storyboard" approach showing consequences of not following procedures.

County Health Department Posters: Search for "(your county) food safety posters" or "kitchen posters" to see how professional kitchens communicate cross-contamination rules visually. For example, here's [Chester County, PA's available posters](https://www.chesco.org/888/Food-Safety-Posters).

## Planning Your Poster

Before you open a design tool, answer these questions:

1. What happened? (Your scenario describes a cross-contamination incident.)
2. What should staff do differently? (This is the core message of your poster.)
3. What's the simplest way to show that? (Visual demonstration beats text explanation.)
